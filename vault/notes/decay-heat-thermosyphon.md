# Idea, thermosyphon for decay-heat removal

This is my own thinking, not established fact.

My idea is to use a passive two-phase closed thermosyphon for the decay-heat removal path so
the design needs no pump on that loop. The appeal is that buoyancy driven natural circulation
keeps removing heat even with no power.

What I am unsure about: whether the Rayleigh margin holds at low power, late in the decay-heat
curve, when the temperature difference driving the loop gets small. If the driving head drops
below what overcomes the loop resistance, circulation could stall exactly when I still need it.

Open questions I want the model to help me reason about:
- Does the literature bound this by the decay-heat curve, and where is the worst case?
- Is there a known dimensionless criterion for stall in two-phase thermosyphons?
- A rejected variant: I considered a forced-flow backup but it defeats the passive goal.
